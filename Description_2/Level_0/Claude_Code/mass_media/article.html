The Invisible Flood: How Groundwater Collapse Is Reshaping the American West

Beneath the cracked earth of California, Arizona, and New Mexico, aquifers that took millennia to fill are being emptied in decades. Our two-year investigation reveals the human cost — and who's profiting from the crisis.

How We Reported This

This investigation is based on two years of fieldwork across California, Arizona, and New Mexico; 12,000 pages of FOIA documents obtained from the EPA, USGS, and three state water agencies; satellite subsidence data from NASA's GRACE mission; and interviews with 47 farmers, hydrologists, regulators, and water-rights attorneys. On the eastern edge of California's San Joaquin Valley, Gonzalo Reyes has been farming alfalfa for thirty-one years. His grandfather drilled the first well in 1952; his father deepened it in 1974. Last September, Gonzalo drilled again — this time to 940 feet, the deepest anyone in his district has gone.

"When I was a kid, water was at 120 feet," he said, standing at the wellhead in the early morning light, squinting against the Central Valley haze. "Now we're chasing it down like a mine shaft. And every year, we have to go deeper."

What's happening beneath Gonzalo's fields is happening across more than 800,000 square miles of the American West. Aquifers that accumulated over thousands of years of slow geological filtration are being pumped faster than they can recharge — in some regions, by a factor of ten or more.

The crisis is invisible because it happens underground, because it moves slowly, and because the agricultural, real estate, and industrial interests that profit most from cheap groundwater access have spent decades funding the political infrastructure to keep it from becoming a front-page story.

Groundwork spent two years tracking that infrastructure. What we found was a network of water lobbying associations, sympathetic state legislators, and strategic campaign contributions that has effectively blocked meaningful groundwater regulation in three of the states most critically affected by depletion — California, Arizona, and New Mexico.

"You can't borrow from an account that was never designed to be a bank. These aquifers took 10,000 years to fill. We've emptied a third of them in 35."
— Dr. Sharon Mehta, USGS Hydrologist

In California, the Sustainable Groundwater Management Act — passed in 2014 after years of advocacy — was supposed to solve this. It required local water agencies to develop plans to bring aquifers to "sustainable" levels by 2040. But the implementation has been captured by the very interests it was meant to constrain.

We obtained 3,400 pages of internal communications from three of California's largest Groundwater Sustainability Agencies through a public records request filed in 2024. The documents show that in at least seven basins — including portions of the Tulare Lake Basin, one of the most depleted in the country — industry representatives were given advance access to draft sustainability plans before they were shared with the public, and in some cases, used that access to water down specific restrictions.

The Arizona story is different but no less troubling. Unlike California, Arizona never passed a comprehensive groundwater protection law for rural areas. The 1980 Groundwater Management Act covers only five urban areas — leaving millions of acres of agricultural and industrial water use essentially unregulated.

"The water that feeds Phoenix's golf courses is the same water a Navajo family will have none of in twenty years. We just haven't connected those dots politically."
— Delores Runningwater, Navajo Water Rights Coalition

In New Mexico, Groundwork found a different mechanism — one that is actually accelerating depletion rather than managing it. The state's water rights system, which dates to Spanish colonial law, allocates water based on "first in time, first in right" — a doctrine that was never designed to account for aquifer collapse.
